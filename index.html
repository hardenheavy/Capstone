The Blues [back to top]

The blues is a genre which originated in the Deep South of the United States around 1860. It quickly evolved. The blues form, ubiquitous in jazz, rhythm and blues and rock and roll, is characterized by the call-and-response pattern, the blues scale and specific chord progressions, of which the twelve-bar blues is the most common. As the blues spread into urban areas and further from the isolated agricultural communities in which it first emerged, musicians adapted their singing and playing in response to their new audiences and to reflect the different communities in which they were living and working. So, sub-genres like Chicago blues, Texas blues, Memphis blues, Blues rock etc. were born.

Through the years, many notable bluesmen such as Muddy Waters, B.B. King, Albert King, John Lee Hooker, Taj Mahal, Robert Cray, Eric Clapton, Stevie Ray Vaughan, Johnny Winter, ZZ Top to name but a few, spread the blues and inspired new generations. Amidst the many sub-genres Blues rock, a fusion genre, started with rock musicians in the UK and the US performing American blues songs with a faster tempo and usually with a more aggressive sound.Blues rock had a rebirth in the early 1990s–2000s, with many artists such as Gary Moore, Kenny Wayne Shepherd, Walter Trout, John Mayer, Jeff Healey, Joe Bonamassa, Eric Gales, Gary Clark Jr., Aynsley Lister any many more.

The Guitars [back to top]

The electric guitar has been part of the blues from as early as the 1930s. The first star of the electric blues is generally recognized as being T-Bone Walker. These days, the electric guitar is as common as it can be, across genres, wielded by “axemen” from all walks of life. From the professional musician to the middle age hobbyist and everyone else in between. Invented in 1932, the electric guitar was adopted by jazz guitar players, who wanted to play single-note guitar solos in large big band ensembles. The popularization of the modern solid-body electric guitar dates back to Leo Fender and Les Paul. Even average music aficionados these days can probably say a thing or two about a Fender Stratocaster or a Gibson Les Paul.

Many players however prefer the simplicity of the Telecaster. With its minimalist design – the thing is basically a plank of wood with neck and strings – it’s as barebones as a design can get. Some of the notable tele bluesmen are Muddy Waters and Albert Collins, although many others also have at least one tele in their arsenal.

Whichever your preference might be, the electric guitar is the bluesmen's best friend, a tool of the trade.
